When you download an app, it usually pops up boxes (kind of like these) asking for your consent in different ways.

An app might ask if it can track where you are in the world while you're using it, listen to you when you're talking to your friends and not using it, or save pictures to your device's gallery when you ask it to.

All of these conversations you've had with these apps are saved in your settings for you to review and change if you'd like.

In reality, you and the Swamp Image Archive are not talking privately.

You're playing a really long game of telephone -- I'm talking through the Swamp Archive, the Swamp Archive is talking to the browser, your browser is talking to your device, your device is talking to a modem, who is talking to a router, who is talking to a bunch of servers all over the world in order to finally get to you.

This isn't even a very accurate descriptions of all the levels of exchanging information involved in getting these words to show up in front of you. It's incredible, but can also be worrisome -- because there are so many people and steps involved, there are things the Swamp Archive just can't guarantee.

You and the Swamp Archive are whispering in a room full of microphones which may or may not be turned on and may or may not be recording.

We don't say this to scare you. You certainly shouldn't feel guilty or responsible for this potential lack of privacy. It isn't your fault for using technology to communicate -- it's one of the main features of most accessible forms of technology.

This isn't coincidental -- people created and regulate these various technologies and systems for communication with and for other people.

It's okay to trust these pieces of technology.

What isn't okay is when those who have created these technologies exploit your body, memories, wants, needs, conversations, touches, etc. for any means except those YOU have agreed to and understand.

You can't know everything about these technologies. You can't even know everything about The Swamp Archive or its true intensions. Sometimes, all you can do is trust the information being given to you.

Everyone is in the same boat. All of this stuff doesn't mean you should stop using technology -- it's a powerful, useful, and incredible tool! Here are some things you can do to at least feel safer:

  • try your best to understand what is being asked of you in terms of service agreements, when apps ask for permissions to be granted, etc.
  • value your own privacy, agency, and autonomy (you should be in charge and in control of you)
  • expect and demand others value and respect the privacy, agency, and autonomy of yourself AND OTHERS!

This is all easier said than done and, as stated before, these problems are not completely your responsibility.

Your data is important and it is an extension of you, but you are not data. You know that. Those who have access and control to your data should too.

The Swamp Archive wants to acknowledge, validate, and value your personhood by respecting your boundaries and your intentions.
